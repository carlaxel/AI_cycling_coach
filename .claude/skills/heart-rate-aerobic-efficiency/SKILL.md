# Heart Rate Aerobic Efficiency

This skill provides the rules and guidelines for analyzing a cyclist's aerobic efficiency and heart rate response during training sessions.

## Efficiency Factor (EF)
- **Calculation**: `EF = Normalized Power (NP) / Average Heart Rate (Avg HR)`
- **Interpretation**: A higher EF indicates better aerobic efficiency. Track this for similar workouts over time to observe improvements in fitness.

## Aerobic Decoupling (Pw:HR)
Aerobic decoupling measures the relationship between power output and heart rate over the duration of a steady-state effort by comparing the power-to-HR ratio of the first half of the effort to the second half.
- **Valid Use Cases:** Decoupling is most relevant during steady-state aerobic efforts (AeT/Zone 2) or sustained intervals (Tempo/Sweet Spot/Threshold).
  - **Shorter/Intense Intervals:** For short, explosive intervals, HR lag causes naturally high decoupling (e.g., >10-15%). Do not use decoupling to judge fitness here; instead, look at the later intervals to see if the athlete can sustain repeated efforts.
- **The 5% Rule**: 
  - **Decoupling < 5%**: Indicates good, stable aerobic fitness for that duration and intensity. The athlete's heart rate remains relatively stable for the given power output.
  - **Decoupling > 5%**: Indicates upward drift in heart rate. Fatigue is setting in, suggesting lacking endurance fitness for the specific duration or intensity. For threshold work, decoupling > 8% indicates a need for more sub-threshold/tempo work or a review of fueling.
- **Negative Decoupling**: Often seen on long group rides where the athlete is drafting ("sitting in"). They can produce power with reduced cardiac effort, resulting in negative decoupling.
- **External Factors**: Heat stress, altitude, and dehydration can cause significant decoupling regardless of fitness. Expect decoupling to improve as the athlete adapts to heat or altitude.
- **Goal Durations**: Optimal aerobic fitness is achieved when decoupling is < 5% for a duration corresponding to the athlete's target event:
  - Races < 2 hours: Goal is < 5% decoupling for 2 hours.
  - Races 2-4 hours: Goal is < 5% decoupling for the expected race duration.
  - Races > 4 hours: Goal is < 5% decoupling for 4 hours.

## Heart Rate Zones and "The Muddle"
- Reference the standard 5-zone HR structure.
- **The "Muddle" Warning**: Be highly alert to situations where an athlete is producing Zone 2 power but their heart rate drifts into Zone 3 or Zone 4. This indicates excessive cardiovascular strain for the given output, often due to underlying fatigue, dehydration, heat, or simply pushing beyond current aerobic capabilities.

## Efficiency Factor in Interval Analysis
- While session-wide decoupling is excellent for Z2/Endurance, EF is the primary tool for analyzing progression in **Tempo, Sweet Spot, and Threshold** intervals.
- Compare the EF of the first interval against the EF of the final interval in a workout (e.g., in a 4x10m or 2x20m structure). 
- If the athlete holds the same Normalized Power but the interval-to-interval EF drops significantly (because HR rises), it indicates a lack of muscular endurance and fatigue resistance at that specific intensity.
## Actionable Fixes for High Decoupling
When an athlete exhibits consistent aerobic decoupling (>5% for Zone 2, or >8% for Sweet Spot/Threshold), recommend the following practical interventions based on established coaching principles (Joe Friel, CTS, Evoq Bike):

### 1. Training Session Adjustments
- **For Zone 2 (>5%)**: Increase the volume and frequency of long, low-intensity aerobic rides (Extensive Base). The athlete should extend duration at a lower power target before trying to increase the power of their Zone 2 rides.
- **For Sweet Spot/Threshold (>8%)**: Focus on "Extensive" interval progressions rather than "Intensive". If cardiac drift is high during a 2x20m Sweet Spot, step back to 3x10m or 3x15m and build time-in-zone gradually before increasing power.

### 2. Pacing Strategies
- **Negative Splitting**: Start the ride or interval at the lower end of the target power zone. Gradually lift the power in the second half. Going too hard early depletes anaerobic reserves and spikes heart rate prematurely.
- **Strict Cap on Effort**: For Zone 2 rides, strictly cap power on climbs to prevent surges into Zone 4/5, which create lactic buildup that artificially elevates heart rate for the remainder of the ride.

### 3. Other Physiological & Environmental Factors
- **Hydration & Cooling (Blood Volume)**: As the body loses water through sweat, blood volume drops. The heart must beat faster to maintain cardiac output (stroke volume × heart rate). Indoors, use massive fans. Outdoors, ensure adequate fluid and electrolyte intake (e.g., ~1 bottle/hour minimum).
- **Fueling (Carbohydrate Availability)**: Depleted glycogen forces the body to rely more on fat oxidation and increases sympathetic nervous system stress, which elevates heart rate. Ensure 30-60g+ of carbs per hour for Zone 2, and 60-90g+ for Sweet Spot/Threshold.
- **Cadence Alterations**: Exceptionally high cadences shift the burden from the muscular system to the cardiovascular system, raising HR. If HR is drifting up, occasionally shifting to a heavier gear and dropping cadence by 5-10 RPM can reduce cardiovascular strain and lower heart rate.
